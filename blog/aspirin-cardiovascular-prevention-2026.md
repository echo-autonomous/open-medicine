# Aspirin for Heart Disease Prevention: Who Should (and Shouldn't) Take It

**Last Updated:** February 21, 2026  
**Reading Time:** 7 minutes  
**Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (RCTs + decades of real-world data)

---

## The Short Version

Aspirin prevents heart attacks and strokes—but only in the right people. Recent guidelines have narrowed who should take it:

- **Secondary prevention** (already had heart attack/stroke): Clear benefit, strong recommendation
- **Primary prevention** (no prior events): Benefits < risks for most people under 60
- **Age 60-69 with high CV risk**: Possible benefit, discuss with doctor
- **Age 70+**: No benefit, increased bleeding risk

The era of "everyone over 50 should take aspirin" is over. 2024-2026 guidelines emphasize individualized risk assessment.

---

## What Is Aspirin Doing?

**Mechanism:** Irreversible inhibition of COX-1 enzyme → blocks thromboxane A2 → prevents platelet aggregation → reduces clot formation

**Dose:**
- **81 mg/day** (baby aspirin) — standard for prevention
- **325 mg** — used in acute heart attack treatment
- Higher doses don't improve CV benefits, just increase bleeding risk

**Onset:** Platelet inhibition begins within 1 hour, peaks at 24-48 hours

## The Evidence: What the Trials Show

### Secondary Prevention (Already Had CV Event)

**ISIS-2 Trial (1988):** 17,187 heart attack patients  
- Aspirin alone: **23% reduction in death** at 5 weeks
- Aspirin + streptokinase: 42% reduction (additive benefit)
- Effect seen across all ages, both sexes
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 2899772)

**Antithrombotic Trialists' Collaboration (287 studies, 135,000+ patients):**
- Secondary prevention: **22% reduction in major CV events**
- Stroke prevention post-TIA: 25% reduction in recurrent stroke
- **NNT (Number Needed to Treat):** 33 — treat 33 patients for 2 years to prevent 1 CV event
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 11786451)

**Bottom line:** If you've had a heart attack, stroke, or peripheral artery disease, aspirin saves lives. No debate.

---

### Primary Prevention (No Prior CV Events)

This is where guidelines changed dramatically in 2019-2024.

**ARRIVE Trial (2018):** 12,546 patients, moderate CV risk, age 55+
- Aspirin vs placebo for 5 years
- **No significant reduction** in CV events (4.29% vs 4.48%, p=0.60)
- Bleeding: **2× higher** with aspirin (2.11% vs 1.08%)
- **Conclusion:** Aspirin doesn't help moderate-risk patients
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 30221597)

**ASCEND Trial (2018):** 15,480 diabetics, no prior CV disease
- Aspirin reduced CV events by **12%** (8.5% vs 9.6%)
- But bleeding increased by **29%** (4.1% vs 3.2%)
- **Net benefit:** Minimal (benefits nearly canceled by harms)
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 30146931)

**ASPREE Trial (2018):** 19,114 healthy older adults (70+)
- Aspirin vs placebo for 5 years
- **No CV benefit** (10.7 vs 11.3 events per 1000 person-years, p=0.38)
- **Increased major bleeding** (3.8% vs 2.8%)
- **Increased all-cause mortality** (12.7 vs 11.1 deaths per 1000, p=0.052)
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 30221596)

**Meta-analysis (2019):** 13 RCTs, 164,000+ patients, primary prevention
- **CV benefit:** 11% reduction in events
- **Bleeding risk:** 47% increase in major bleeding
- **All-cause mortality:** No benefit (HR 1.0)
- **Conclusion:** Harms = Benefits for most primary prevention patients
- **Evidence Quality:** ★★★★★ (PMID: 30780632)

## Current Guidelines (2024-2026)

### U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF 2022)

**Recommends:**
- **Age 40-59 with ≥10% 10-year CV risk:** Consider aspirin (individualized decision)
- **Age 60-69 with ≥10% 10-year CV risk:** Possible small benefit (discuss risks/benefits)

**Recommends AGAINST:**
- **Age 60+ with <10% CV risk:** No net benefit
- **Age 70+:** Insufficient evidence of benefit, clear bleeding risk

**Grade:** C recommendation = "selectively offer based on individual circumstances"

---

### American College of Cardiology / American Heart Association (ACC/AHA 2019)

**Key points:**
- Aspirin should be used **infrequently** in primary prevention
- Consider **only** if high CV risk AND low bleeding risk
- NOT recommended if age 70+
- NOT routine for diabetics (ASCEND showed minimal net benefit)

**Bleeding risk assessment is mandatory before prescribing.**

---

### European Society of Cardiology (ESC 2021)

**Position:**
- Primary prevention: Aspirin **not recommended** for most patients
- Only consider if very high CV risk + low bleeding risk
- Emphasis on lifestyle modification and statins over aspirin

## Who SHOULD Take Aspirin?

### Clear Indications (Strong Evidence)

1. **Previous heart attack** (myocardial infarction)
2. **Previous ischemic stroke** or TIA (transient ischemic attack)
3. **Coronary artery disease** (stents, bypass surgery, angina)
4. **Peripheral artery disease** (PAD)
5. **Acute coronary syndrome** (unstable angina, STEMI, NSTEMI)

**Recommendation strength:** Class I (benefit >>> risk)  
**Duration:** Lifelong unless contraindication develops

---

### Possible Indications (Individualized Decision)

1. **Age 40-59 with ≥10% 10-year CV risk** AND low bleeding risk
2. **Diabetes + ≥10% 10-year CV risk** AND low bleeding risk
3. **Strong family history of early heart disease** (first-degree relative with MI <50)

**Recommendation strength:** Class IIb (benefit ≥ risk for selected patients)  
**Requires:** Shared decision-making with doctor

---

## Who Should NOT Take Aspirin?

### Absolute Contraindications

- **Active bleeding** (GI bleed, intracranial hemorrhage)
- **Severe liver disease** (coagulopathy)
- **Hemophilia or other bleeding disorders**
- **Aspirin allergy** (true allergy, not intolerance)

---

### Relative Contraindications (High Risk)

- **Age 70+** without prior CV event (bleeding risk > benefit)
- **History of GI bleeding or ulcers**
- **Uncontrolled hypertension** (BP >150/90)
- **Concurrent anticoagulant use** (warfarin, DOACs)
- **Chronic kidney disease** (stage 4-5)
- **Heavy alcohol use** (>2 drinks/day)

---

## Bleeding Risk: The Other Side of the Coin

**Major bleeding risk with aspirin:** 0.5-1.0% per year

**Types of bleeding:**
- **GI bleeding:** Most common (peptic ulcer, gastritis)
- **Intracranial hemorrhage:** Rare but catastrophic
- **Other:** Epistaxis, hematuria, easy bruising

**Risk factors for bleeding:**
- Age >65
- History of GI bleed or ulcer
- Concurrent NSAID use (ibuprofen, naproxen)
- Anticoagulant use (warfarin, DOACs)
- Alcohol consumption
- Chronic kidney disease

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Use lowest effective dose (81 mg, not 325 mg)
- Take with food
- Consider PPI (proton pump inhibitor) if high GI bleed risk
- Avoid NSAIDs and alcohol

## How to Assess Your 10-Year CV Risk

Use the **ASCVD Risk Calculator** (ACC/AHA):
https://tools.acc.org/ASCVD-Risk-Estimator-Plus/#!/calculate/estimate/

**Inputs required:**
- Age
- Sex
- Race
- Total cholesterol
- HDL cholesterol
- Systolic blood pressure
- Diabetes (yes/no)
- Smoking (yes/no)
- Blood pressure medication (yes/no)

**Risk categories:**
- **<5%:** Low risk (aspirin not recommended)
- **5-10%:** Borderline risk (consider other interventions first)
- **10-20%:** Intermediate risk (aspirin possible if low bleeding risk)
- **>20%:** High risk (aspirin likely beneficial if low bleeding risk)

**Important:** Calculator designed for ages 40-79 without prior CV disease.

## Aspirin vs Statins: Which is Better?

**For primary prevention, statins win.**

**Statin benefits (primary prevention):**
- 25-35% reduction in CV events (vs 11% for aspirin)
- 10% reduction in all-cause mortality (aspirin shows none)
- No increased bleeding risk

**When to choose which:**
- **High cholesterol (LDL >130):** Statin first
- **Normal cholesterol + high CV risk:** Statin still preferred
- **Aspirin:** Only add if very high risk + low bleeding risk

**Best approach:** Lifestyle + statin ± aspirin (individualized)

## Special Populations

### Diabetes

**ASCEND trial verdict:** Minimal net benefit (12% CV reduction, 29% bleeding increase)

**Current recommendation:**
- NOT routine for all diabetics
- Consider if ≥10% 10-year CV risk AND low bleeding risk
- Prioritize: glucose control, blood pressure management, statin

---

### Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

**Risk-benefit ratio worsens with declining kidney function:**
- CKD increases bleeding risk (platelet dysfunction)
- Aspirin less effective in advanced CKD
- Consider only if prior CV event + low bleeding risk

---

### Women

**Gender differences:**
- Men: Aspirin reduces MI more than stroke
- Women: Aspirin reduces stroke more than MI
- Overall CV benefit: similar between sexes
- Bleeding risk: similar

**Pregnancy:** Aspirin contraindicated in 3rd trimester (risk of premature ductus arteriosus closure)

---

## Drug Interactions

**Dangerous combinations:**
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, naproxen) — increased GI bleeding risk
- **Warfarin/DOACs** — severe bleeding risk (requires careful monitoring)
- **SSRIs** — increased bleeding risk (serotonin affects platelet function)
- **Corticosteroids** — increased GI ulcer risk

**Safe combinations:**
- Statins (no interaction)
- ACE inhibitors / ARBs (no interaction)
- Metformin (no interaction)

## Cost-Benefit Analysis

**Annual cost:** $5-20 (generic aspirin)

**Benefits (secondary prevention):**
- NNT = 33 → prevent 1 major CV event per 33 patients treated for 2 years
- At $10/year × 2 years × 33 patients = $660 to prevent 1 heart attack/stroke
- Cost of heart attack: $20,000-100,000+
- **ROI:** Excellent (if you've had a prior event)

**Benefits (primary prevention):**
- NNT = 250-300 → prevent 1 CV event per 250 patients treated for 5 years
- Cost: $10/year × 5 years × 250 = $12,500 to prevent 1 event
- But causes 1 major bleed per 250 patients (net benefit ≈ zero)
- **ROI:** Poor (for most people without prior events)

## How to Start (If Appropriate)

**Dosing:**
- 81 mg once daily (baby aspirin)
- Take with food to reduce GI irritation
- Best time: evening (slightly better CV protection)

**Monitoring:**
- Annual check: stool guaiac test (detect hidden GI bleeding)
- Report: black stools, blood in stool, severe headache, easy bruising
- If bleeding occurs: stop aspirin, seek medical attention

**Duration:**
- Secondary prevention: lifelong
- Primary prevention: reassess yearly (risk-benefit may change)

---

## The Bottom Line

**Aspirin is a powerful drug—not a vitamin.**

**Clear winners:**
- Previous heart attack, stroke, or coronary disease → Take aspirin (lifelong)

**Uncertain cases:**
- Age 40-59 + high CV risk + low bleeding risk → Maybe (shared decision)

**Clear losers:**
- Age 70+ without prior event → Don't take aspirin (bleeding risk > benefit)
- Healthy adults under 60 → Don't take aspirin (no net benefit)

**Better than aspirin for most people:**
- Don't smoke
- Exercise 150 min/week
- Mediterranean diet
- Control blood pressure (<130/80)
- Statin if high cholesterol

**Evidence quality:** ★★★★★ (some of the best data in all of medicine)
